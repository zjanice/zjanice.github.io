HydrateMe

Public display application with social networking feature to motivate people to drink more water

Background

The findings show that most people do not drink the required amount of water that they should be drinking daily. Many people only drink water when they are thirsty. But the problem is that thirst doesn’t immediately reveal the body’s need for water. So our goal is to help people be aware of their water consumption and drink the adequate amount of water that their body needs; identify how technology can be used as an effective strategy to address this issue. ​

This project explores the use of a public display platform to motivate office workers to drink the healthy amount of water and help them track and measure their water consumption. It uses social persuasion as a means to remind the users of their water consumption including the water in the foods and snacks users eat, and offers the users health tips in order to persuade them in making better choices regarding the types of foods/drinks they consume.



Challenges

How to ideate and narrow down our design options? ​

We did literature review of 20 papers and conducted competitive analysis of current systems, either commercial products or research projects. Also we conducted ethnography study and research. After that, the product requirements became clear. A simplified version of our process went as follows:
​

  • - We made several rounds of brainstorming and generated scenarios and personas.
  • - We built up medium-fidelity mockups and did pilot test with users and then moved to the development stage.
  • - We narrowed down and finalized our idea and polished interface

Building Up

System Design

HydrateMe is designed as a public display, which would be placed in a public area in an office workplace, such as kitchen. Based on the ethnographic observations, we determined functional and non-functional requirements of our system.


Design Iteration and Implementation

Paper Prototyping : After running the paper prototyping session, we tried to find out some of the notions that the users were not able to recognize in the system and changed them so that they would be closer to their mental model.


Heuristic Evaluation helped us identify some unexpected problems. For example, some male users expressed that the interface was not very suitable for them and they found the design more suitable for female users. Therefore we changed the idea of a garden full of flowers, to a forest full of monsters in order to make it more playful and gender unbiased.
